Max: And the two uses that tend to fly under the radar are neither as an adjective and 
neither as a pronoun. Which, honestly, sound way more complicated than they are. 
Mia: Let's start with the adjective one, because it's probably the simpler of the two. When 
you use neither as an adjective, you're placing it directly before a singular noun, and what 
you're doing is saying "not one, not the other." Both options are off the table. 
Max: So for example, imagine there's an election and you're not exactly thrilled about your 
choices. You could say "Neither candidate is good." You're not picking sides. You're rejecting

"Neither shoe fits me." Always singular, always right before the noun. 
Mia: And that's the key thing to remember, singular. It trips a lot of people up because 
logically you're talking about two things, but the noun that follows neither is always singular. 
Max: Now, the pronoun use is a little different. Here, neither stands on its own, it replaces 
the noun instead of sitting next to it. And this usually happens when the context is already

[NEW TOPIC: "So do I" vs. "Neither do I"] 
Max: Okay new topic. And this one connects directly to what we've been talking about, so 
the timing works out pretty well. 
Mia: We're looking at short agreements. Specifically, "So do I" and "Neither do I" and why 
getting them right is actually trickier than it sounds. 
Max: So the basic idea is this: when someone says something and you feel the same way, 
you want to agree without just repeating everything they said. That's where these structures 
come in. But here's where people stumble, the auxiliary verb you use has to match the tense 
of what the other person said. You can't just pick one and stick with it forever. 
Mia: Let's walk through it. If someone says "I love pizza" present simple you mirror that with 
"So do I." The auxiliary is do because that's what present simple uses. Now if they said "I 
went to Paris" past simple you'd say "So did I." Not "So do I." The tense shifts, so the 
auxiliary shifts with it. 
Max: And it's not just regular tenses. If someone says "I can swim" you're dealing with a 
modal verb now so you carry that modal over: "So can I." Same logic applies to should, 
would, will, have whatever is in their sentence is what you're working with. 
Mia: Now flip it to the negative side. If someone says "I'm not tired" that's a negative 
statement with to be your agreement would be "Neither am I." Not "Neither do I." The verb to 
be doesn't use do, so neither should your response. 
Max: And that's really the core of it you're not memorizing fixed phrases, you're learning to 
listen closely to what someone says and reflect the right auxiliary back. Once that clicks, it 
becomes instinct.

[NEW TOPIC: "Wish" and "If only"] 
Mia: Okay, last topic of the day and this one hits a little differently because it's not just 
grammar, it's emotion. 
Max: We're talking about wish and if only. And these two are essentially expressing the 
same thing a desire for something to be different. The difference is mostly intensity. If only 
tends to carry more weight, more feeling. But the grammar behind both works the same way. 
Mia: Here's the part that catches people off guard even though you're talking about the 
present, you use a past tense verb. So if right now, today, you don't have enough time and 
you wish you did you'd say "I wish I had more time." Not "I wish I have." The situation is 
present, but the structure reaches back into the past tense to signal that it's not real. It's 
something you want but don't have. 
Max: And if only works exactly the same way. "If only I had more time." Same meaning, just 
a bit more dramatic. Which, depending on the situation, is completely valid. 
Mia: Now when you're talking about the past, about something that already happened and 
can't be changed, that's where regret lives. And for that, you go one step further past perfect. 
So instead of just past simple, you're using had plus the past participle. "I wish I hadn't said 
that." "If only I had listened." The thing already happened, and you're looking back at it 
wishing it had gone differently. 
Max: So to keep it clean present situation you want to change: past simple. Past situation 
you regret: past perfect. The further back the feeling goes, the further back the grammar 
goes with it. 
Mia: And honestly, once you start noticing this structure, you'll hear it everywhere. People 
use it all the time without realizing it's even a grammar point.
