🎙️ SECTION 3: THE SECRET OF NATIVE SPEAKERS 
(CONNECTED SPEECH) 
RACHEL: Yes. The biggest frustration every English learner faces. The reason why you can 
read a perfectly complex English book, but when you watch an American movie, it sounds 
like an alien language. 
MATTHEW: The dreaded connected speech. Or as a lot of people call it, speaking glued 
together. 
RACHEL: Speaking glued together is the perfect way to describe it! Because when we write, 
every single word has a clear space before and after it. But when native speakers actually 
talk... we completely ignore those spaces. We glue words together to save time and energy. 
MATTHEW: We are incredibly lazy with our mouths. We do not speak word by word. We 
speak in chunks. And if you are trying to listen to an English speaker, and your brain is 
waiting to hear every single individual word clearly, you are going to fall behind instantly. 
RACHEL: Let's give them a really obvious example. Let's take the question: What are you 
going to do? If you read it on a piece of paper, it is six separate, distinct words. What. Are. 
You. Going. To. Do. 
MATTHEW: But if we are hanging out at a coffee shop on a Friday afternoon, I am never, 
ever going to pronounce all six of those words perfectly. I would sound like a robot. 
RACHEL: Exactly. Instead, I'm going to mash them all together. "What are you" becomes 
"Whatcha". "Going to" becomes "gonna". So the whole, long sentence shrinks down into 
three quick beats. Whatcha gonna do? Whatcha gonna do? 
MATTHEW: Listeners, shadow that right now. Don't think about the spelling. Just match the 
sound. Whatcha gonna do? 
RACHEL: Notice how your jaw stays pretty relaxed. You don't have to work hard to say it. 
And this glueing process happens everywhere. It's not just slang. It's a fundamental rule of 
English pronunciation. 
MATTHEW: Let's talk about consonant-to-vowel linking. This is a huge one. If a word ends in 
a consonant sound, and the very next word begins with a vowel sound, they become one 
single word. 
RACHEL: Like magnets pulling each other. 
MATTHEW: Exactly, like magnets. For example, if I want to say "an apple", I do not stop the 
air flowing out of my mouth between the 'n' and the 'a'. I don't say "an....apple". That pause

RACHEL: Or think about the phrase "grab it". It is not "grab... it". The 'b' slides right over into

🎙️ SECTION 4: THE STEP-BY-STEP ROUTINE 
RACHEL: This is so important. Because I see learners getting super motivated, they put on 
a two-hour Hollywood movie, they try to shadow the main character for the whole film, and 
after ten minutes their brain melts and they give up forever. 
MATTHEW: Please do not do that. Shadowing a full movie is like trying to run a marathon on 
your first day of jogging. You will exhaust yourself and lose your voice. You need a system. 
You need to do short, focused sets, just like at the gym. 
RACHEL: Step number one of the routine is finding your material. And this is a 
make-or-break decision. You have to choose audio of someone speaking the way you 
actually want to speak in your real life. 
MATTHEW: Right. If your goal is to have casual, relaxed conversations with friends or 
coworkers, do not practice shadowing a sixty-year-old politician giving a formal speech to 
parliament. You will end up sounding incredibly weird at parties. 
RACHEL:  "Good evening, mate, I formally request a slice of pizza." No! Pick a YouTube 
vlogger, a lifestyle podcast, or an interview with an actor you really like. Someone who is just 
chatting naturally. 
MATTHEW: And here is the golden rule: you only need a clip that is about thirty seconds to 
one minute long. That is all. One minute of audio contains enough pronunciation lessons to 
keep you busy for a whole week. 
RACHEL: So, you have your one-minute clip. Step number two is what I call the Blind 
Listening phase. Do not look at the subtitles. Do not look at the transcript. Just close your 
_________________________________________________________________________

eyes, press play, and listen to that same one minute of audio three, four, or five times in a 
row. 
MATTHEW: You are acting like a musician listening to a new song. You are listening for the 
rhythm. Where does the speaker get loud? Where do they drop their voice and whisper? 
Where do they use that connected speech we talked about? Where do they say "wanna" 
instead of "want to"? 
RACHEL: Once you feel the rhythm of the clip, we move to step number three. And 
Matthew, I think you call this the mumbling phase, right? 
MATTHEW:  Yes, the mumbling phase. It looks completely ridiculous if someone watches 
you doing it, but it is highly effective. You play the audio again, but this time, you just hum or 
mumble along with the speaker. 
RACHEL: Don't try to form the actual English words yet! 
MATTHEW: Exactly. Just keep your mouth slightly closed and match their pitch. If their voice 
goes up, your mumble goes up. Hmm-hmm-HMM-hmm. You are removing the pressure of 
vocabulary and grammar, and you are purely internalizing the music and the emotion of the 
sentence. 
RACHEL: It really removes the anxiety. And then, finally, we get to step number four. The 
actual, full shadowing. This is when you bring the text in. You can look at the transcript now, 
but you have to promise not to stare at it too hard. 
MATTHEW: The text is just a safety net. You play the audio, and you speak right on top of 
the native speaker, or just a microsecond behind them. Let your ears guide your mouth, not 
your eyes. If the text says "going to" but your ears clearly hear "gonna", you say "gonna". 
Always trust your ears over the text. 
RACHEL: And you just loop that one-minute clip over and over. You do it five times, ten 
times, until your mouth moves automatically without you having to force it. Until it feels easy.

🎙️ SECTION 5: GOLDEN RULES AND COMMON 
MISTAKES 
MATTHEW: Now, while you are doing this routine, there are a couple of golden rules you 
have to keep in mind, otherwise you are just making noise without actually learning. Rachel, 
what is the biggest mistake you see people make when they start shadowing? 
RACHEL: Parroting. Just repeating words like a parrot without actually understanding the 
context or the emotion behind them. 
MATTHEW: Oh, yes. The empty echo. 
_________________________________________________________________________ 
MAX & MIA PODCAST 
 
RACHEL: Exactly. If you don't know what the sentence means, you cannot possibly replicate 
the correct emotion. And intonation is entirely driven by emotion. If the speaker is being 
sarcastic, their voice moves in a very specific way. If they are excited, it moves differently. If 
you are just blindly repeating sounds without feeling the meaning, your brain won't retain the 
pattern. 
MATTHEW: So Rule number one: Always understand the vocabulary and the context of the 
clip before you start shadowing it. Translate the words if you have to. Understand the joke. 
Feel the emotion. 
RACHEL: And Rule number two: You have to record yourself. 
MATTHEW: The one thing everyone hates doing. 
RACHEL:  I know, I know! Nobody likes the sound of their own voice on a recording. It is 
incredibly uncomfortable at first. But you have to do it. Your brain lies to you while you are 
speaking. In your head, you might think you sound exactly like Brad Pitt or Emma Stone. But 
when you play the recording back, you realize, "Oh, wait, I didn't connect those words at all, 
and my 'r' sound is completely flat." 
MATTHEW: It is the only objective way to get feedback if you don't have a personal tutor 
sitting next to you. Use the voice memo app on your phone. Record the original speaker, and 
then record yourself doing the exact same line. Play them back-to-back. The differences will 
be brutally obvious, but that is exactly how you fix them. 
RACHEL: It's self-correction. And it is the fastest track to fluency. You don't have to record 
every single session, but try to do it at least once a week so you can actually hear your own 
progress. Because trust me, after a month of consistent shadowing, you will sound 
completely different.
