VOCABULARY & LANGUAGE 
Max: Let's start with the obvious one. The most common thing you'd say is just "Can you 
cover my shift?" Short, direct, everyone understands it. That works perfectly fine between 
colleagues who know each other well. 
Mia: Right, and there's also "I need to swap shifts" which is slightly different, because 
swapping implies you're offering your shift in return for theirs. It's more of an exchange. 
Max: Good point. And then you've got phrases like "something came up" which is one of 
those expressions that's incredibly useful because it explains the situation without you 
having to go into detail. 
Mia: Although it's worth being a little careful with that one. If you use it too often, people start 
to notice. 
Max:  Fair enough. Now one thing that makes a real difference is how you frame the ask. 
Saying "Can you cover my shift?" is fine, but "Would you be available to take my shift?" 
sounds a bit more considerate. You're giving the other person room to say no. 
Mia: And always always offer something back. "I'll cover for you next time" or "I'll return the 
favour" goes a long way. It shows you're not just asking for a handout. 
Max: Exactly. Now, if you want to take it up a notch say you're writing to your manager, or it's 
a more formal workplace the language shifts a little. Instead of "something came up," you 
might say "an unexpected situation has come up on my end." It's the same idea, just a bit 
more polished.

Mia: "I'd like to cancel my membership effective immediately" is a step up "effective
