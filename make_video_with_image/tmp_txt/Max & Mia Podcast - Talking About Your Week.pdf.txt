The "Bad Week" Toolkit & The Setup 
Matthew: Alright, let’s get into the nitty-gritty. Let’s talk about when things go wrong. 
Rachel: Or just... when life gets overwhelming. We all have those weeks where we just want 
to crawl back into bed on Monday morning, right? 
Matthew: Definitely. But here’s the thing: If someone asks "How’s your week?" and you just 
say "Bad" or "Busy," you sound... well, a bit like a robot. 
Rachel: Beep boop. "I am busy." Exactly! We want to paint a picture. So, instead of "busy," 
try saying: "I’m snowed under." 
Matthew: Oh, that’s a classic. It means you have so much work covering you, like snow, that 
you can’t move. Another good one is when work just keeps coming and coming... we say the 
work is piling up. 
Rachel: Ugh, I hate when work piles up. And if the week is just slow, boring, and feels 
endless? You can say: "It’s such a drag." 
Matthew: "A drag." Great word. It sounds exactly like what it means. Now, before we listen 
to our first conversation, we need to address a huge grammar trap. 
Rachel: The famous ED vs ING adjectives. This is the quickest way to spot if someone is B1 
or B2. 
_________________________________________________________________________ 
MAX & MIA PODCAST 
 
Matthew: Right. Remember: ED is for how you feel. ING is for the situation. 
Rachel: So, please, don’t say "My week was very stressed." The week doesn't have 
feelings! The week was stressful. You were stressed. 
Matthew: Or "I am very boring." No, no, no! Unless you are a really uninteresting person! 
You mean "I am bored" because the week is boring. 
Rachel: Small change, big difference. Okay, I think our listeners are ready for a real 
example. 
Matthew: I think so too. We have a recording of two friends. They are both having a terrible

Rachel: But here is the challenge for you guys listening: One of them is speaking at a B1

The Breakdown & The "Winning Week" 
Matthew: Wow. Poor Phil, right? You could really feel the difference there. 
Rachel: Absolutely. Phil was perfectly understandable—he communicated his 
problems—but he sounded a bit repetitive. "I am tired," "It is busy," "It is bad." 
Matthew: Exactly. It’s what we call "flat" English. But Angela? She brought the color. Did you 
catch that word she used instead of "very tired"? 
Rachel: "Shattered." I love that word. It sounds exactly like how you feel when you’re 
broken into pieces from exhaustion. 
Matthew: And she didn't just say "I have a lot of work." She said the work was "piling up." 
Rachel: That is a great Phrasal Verb. You can visualize the papers getting higher and higher 
on the desk. That is the power of B2 English—it creates images in the listener's mind. 
Matthew: Okay, enough complaining! We’ve covered the "bad week." But what if you are 
having an amazing week? What if you are crushing it? 
Rachel: Please, please, do not say: "My week is very good." We can do better! 
Matthew: If everything is going according to plan, with no problems, you can say: "It’s been 
smooth sailing." 
Rachel: Like a boat on a calm ocean. "Smooth sailing." I like that. Or, if you are getting a lot 
of things done efficiently, you can say: "I’m on a roll." 
Matthew: "On a roll." That means you are unstoppable. You are finishing one task, then the 
next, then the next. 
Rachel: Also, pay attention to the grammar here. When we talk about a good week, we 
often focus on achievements. So, you will hear a lot of the Present Perfect. 
Matthew: Right. Instead of "I finished the report" (Past Simple), you might hear: "I’ve finally 
managed to finish that report." It emphasizes the success of the action. 
Rachel: Exactly. So, let’s see this in action. We have another conversation for you. 
Matthew: Spoiler alert: They are having a much better week than Angela and Phil.

The "Good Week" Analysis 
 
Matthew: Okay, I have to say, the vibe in that conversation was completely different. It’s 
impossible not to smile when you hear someone having such a good week. 
Rachel: Totally! And notice how smooth the conversation was? Ryan understood everything 
Cathy threw at him—even the idioms. 
Matthew: Exactly. And that is a key part of being a solid B1 speaker. You might not use all 
the idioms yourself yet, but you understand them from context. 
Rachel: Right. But let's look at the difference in how they spoke. Cathy used really colorful 
language. Did you catch "smooth sailing"? 
Matthew: Yes! "It was smooth sailing." That’s such a great visual. Imagine a boat on calm 
water. No storms, no waves. Just easy progress. 
Rachel: It’s much more descriptive than just saying "It was easy." And another one I really 
liked was when Cathy said her hard work finally "paid off." 
Matthew: Oh, that’s a crucial phrasal verb. To pay off. It means your effort produced a good 
result. It was worth it. Ryan responded with: "It is always a great feeling when you finish a 
big job." Which is perfect English...

The Final Polish (Transition & Transformation) 
Matthew: So, before we sign off, how about we give them a little "cheat sheet"? Let’s do a 
quick round of "Boring vs. Brilliant." I’ll give you a standard textbook sentence—the kind 
we want to avoid—and you give me the B2 upgrade. Just to get everyone’s creative juices 
flowing. 
Rachel: Ooh, I love a makeover. Let’s do it. Give me your worst. 
Matthew: Alright. Imagine it’s Friday afternoon, it’s been a long week. The boring sentence 
is: "I am very tired." 
Rachel: Hmm. "I am very tired." It’s okay, grammatically correct... but dull. If I really want to 
show how drained I am, I’d say: "I’m running on fumes." 
Matthew: "Running on fumes." That is such a vivid image. 
Rachel: Right? It’s like a car that has zero petrol left. You’re still moving, but the engine is 
coughing. It’s much more dramatic than just "tired." 
_________________________________________________________________________

Matthew: Love it. Okay, next one. Let’s try a work scenario. You’re inviting me to lunch, but I 
can't go. The robot answer is: "I cannot come because I am busy." 
Rachel: Ugh, sounds so cold! If you want to sound professional but occupied, try saying: 
"I’m a bit tied up right now." 
Matthew: "Tied up." Like with a rope? 
Rachel: Exactly! It creates the mental image that you literally cannot leave your desk 
because the work is holding you there. "Sorry, I’m tied up with a client." It sounds much 
softer and more natural. 
Matthew: That’s a great tip. Okay, let’s flip it. Something positive. You just got amazing 
news. The boring sentence is: "I am very happy." 
Rachel: We can definitely do better than that. I think I’d say: "I’m absolutely chuffed." 
Matthew: Ah, "chuffed." That’s very British, isn’t it? 
Rachel: It is! But you hear it everywhere now. It means you’re really pleased with yourself or 
a result. It feels warm and genuine. Like, "I finally finished that project, I’m absolutely 
chuffed." 
Matthew: Nice. Okay, last one. This is for the weekend coming up. The boring sentence is: 
"I want to relax." 
Rachel: We all want to relax. But to make it sound deeper, I would say: "I need to unwind." 
Matthew: "Unwind." Like a clock? 
Rachel: Yes! Imagine you are a toy or a clock that has been wound up too tight. You are 
tense. To relax, you need to un-wind. You need to release that tension. 
Matthew: So, not just sitting on the sofa, but actually letting go of the stress. 
Rachel: Precisely. So, instead of "I’m going to relax," tell us "I’m going to unwind with a good 
book." It sounds much more therapeutic. 
Matthew: I feel more relaxed just hearing it. See? That’s the power of choosing the right 
word. You don't need long, complicated sentences. You just need the right mental image.
