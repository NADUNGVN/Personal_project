How to Argue Politely in English 
Max: So let's start with something basic. When most people have a disagreement in English, 
what's the first word that comes out? 
Mia: "No." 
Max: Exactly. Just "no." Or even worse — silence, because they don't know what to say. And 
look, there's nothing wrong with "no" — but in a real conversation, especially a tense one, 
just saying "no" can come across as rude, even if that's not your intention at all. 
Mia: And that's the thing about English — tone and word choice matter a lot. You can say 
the exact same thing in two different ways and one sounds completely reasonable while the 
other sounds like you're starting a fight. 
Max: So let's talk about some key phrases. Things you can actually use. 
Mia: Right. So first — instead of just saying "no, you're wrong" — which, again, sounds 
pretty aggressive — you can say something like: "I see where you're coming from, but…" or 
"I understand your point, however…" 
_________________________________________________________________________ 
MAX & MIA PODCAST 
 
Max: Those two little phrases do a lot of work. They show you actually listened to the other 
person before responding. And that goes a long way. 
Mia: Another one that's really useful: "With all due respect…" — now, be careful with this 
one. In English, this phrase is a way of softening something you're about to say that might be 
uncomfortable. But if you say it the wrong way, it can actually sound sarcastic. 
Max: Yeah, tone is everything with that one. 
Mia: So practice it. Say it calmly, not dramatically. "With all due respect, I don't think that's 
quite accurate." 
Max: Now let's talk about something that a lot of learners struggle with — and this is more of 
a grammar point. When you're disagreeing, there's a big difference between saying "You are 
wrong" and "I think there might be a misunderstanding." 
Mia: Huge difference. 
Max: The first one puts the blame directly on the other person. The second one opens a 
door — it suggests maybe neither of you has the full picture. And in English, especially in 
professional or formal situations, that kind of language — what we call "hedging" — is really 
important. 
Mia: Hedging. Good word. So hedging means softening your statements so they don't sound 
too absolute. Instead of "This is wrong" you say "I'm not sure this is entirely correct." Instead 
of "You didn't understand me" you say "I think I might not have explained myself clearly 
enough." 
Max: And that last one is actually a great trick — turning it back on yourself, even when 
you're right. It defuses tension immediately. 
Mia: Now, let's talk about vocabulary for a second — because this is something that really 
separates a good English speaker from a great one. A lot of learners get stuck using the 
same words over and over. "Bad", "good", "big problem", "I don't agree"… 
Max: Which are fine, but they don't really paint a picture. 
Mia: Exactly. So let's upgrade some of those. Instead of "That's a big problem" — try "That's 
a significant issue" or "That raises a serious concern." Instead of "I don't agree" — try "I beg 
to differ" or "I'm not entirely convinced by that." 
Max: And instead of "That's wrong" — you could say "I think that might be a misconception" 
or "That doesn't quite hold up." 
Mia: See how much more natural and composed that sounds? You're saying the same thing, 
but the way you say it makes you sound calmer, more confident, more in control of the 
situation. 
_________________________________________________________________________

Max: Which brings us to probably the most common mistake — and it's not really a grammar 
mistake, it's more of a habit. People tend to over-explain when they're nervous. They start a 
sentence, then go back, then add more, then apologize mid-sentence… 
Mia: "Sorry, I mean, what I wanted to say is, well, it's not that I disagree completely, I just…" 
Max: And by the time you've finished, you've kind of lost the argument just from the way you 
said it. 
Mia: So the tip here is: short, clear sentences. State your point. Let it land. You don't need to 
fill every silence.
